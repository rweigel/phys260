1 Introduction

5

A stationary or moving charge q in an electric field (created by other charges) experiences a force of

7

F=qE

9

A moving charge creates a magnetic field. A moving charge q in a magnetic field (created by other moving charges) experiences a force of

11

F=qv×B

13

The direction of the force on q is perpendicular to a plane that contains v and B. This plane can be visualized by imagining orienting a flat piece of paper such that both the v and B vectors lie on the paper. The direction of v×B is perpendicular to the paper. As discussed in the Electric Flux activity, there are two perpendicular directions for a plane; the one to choose is determined by the right–hand rule for cross products.

15

The magnitude of F can be computed using the above equation to find Fx​, Fy​, and Fz​ and then F=Fx2​+Fy2​+Fz2​​. Or, one can use

17

F=∣q∣vBsinϕ,

19

where ϕ is the angle between v and B and 0≤ϕ≤180∘. (For example, the angle between two vectors can be said to be ϕ=10∘ or ϕ=350∘. In the above equation, use 10∘; if 350∘ was used, you would get a negative value for the magnitude of F, which is positive by definition because it is a magnitude.)

21

There are two special cases for which the general equation F=qv×B simplifies to a very simple equation:

23

  1. If the direction of motion of q is perpendicular to the magnetic field (so that ϕ=90∘), the magnitude of the force is F=qvB and the direction of F is determined by the right–hand rule.

    25

  2. If the direction of motion is parallel or antiparallel to the magnetic field (so that ϕ=0∘ or ϕ=180∘), the magnitude of the force is zero.

    26
25-26

When the vectors in a cross product are both in a Cartesian coordinate plane (so that the angle ϕ between them is easily computed), the formula F=∣q∣vBsinϕ can be used to find the magnitude of F and its direction is given by the right–hand rule. Otherwise, finding the angle ϕ and the perpendicular direction requires a diagram and several steps of trigonometry. In this case, using vector notation to compute F is easier.

28

3 Trajectories

167

If the magnetic force on a charge particle is not zero the direction of its velocity vector will change. Because the magnetic force depends on the velocity vector direction, the magnetic force vector will also change.

169

For a charged particle moving in a region of space with a constant magnetic field (both in magnitude and direction), there are three general trajectories:

171

  1. If the velocity is parallel to the magnetic field lines, the trajectory is a straight line because no magnetic forces are acting on it.

    173

  2. If velocity is perpendicular to the magnetic field lines, the trajectory is spiral. The particle rotates in a circle in the same plane as the velocity vector and drifts parallel or antiparallel to the field lines.

    174

  3. The velocity has parallel and perpendicular components to the magnetic field line. In this case, the particle will move at a constant speed parallel or antiparallel to the field lines and in a circle around the field line. The trajectory is a spiral.

    175
173-175

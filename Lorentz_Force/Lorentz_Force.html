Lorentz Force

1 Introduction

Charges create an electric field. The charges that create the electric field can be stationary or moving.

A stationary or moving charge q in an electric field (created by other charges) experiences a force of

F=qE

A magnetic field is created by moving charges. A stationary charge does not create a magnetic field. A moving charge q in a magnetic field (created by other moving charges) experiences a force of

F=qv×B

The direction of the force on q is perpendicular to a plane that contains v and B. This plane can be visualized by imagining orienting a piece of paper such that both the v and B vectors lie on the paper. See Figure 27.6 of the textbook.

The magnitude of F can be computed using the above equation or

F=∣q∣vBsinϕ

where ϕ is the angle between v and B and ϕ≤180∘. (For example, the angle between two vectors can be said to be 10∘ or 350∘. In the above equation, use 10∘ – if 350∘ was used, we would get a negative value for the magnitude of F, which is positive by definition because it is a magnitude.)

There are two special cases for which the general equation F=qv×B simplifies to a very simple equation:

  1. If the direction of motion of q is perpendicular to the magnetic field (so that ϕ=90∘), the magnitude of the force is F=qvB and the direction is determined by right-hand-rule.

  2. If the direction of motion is parallel or anti-parallel to the magnetic field (so that ϕ=0∘ or ϕ=180∘), the magnitude of the force is F=0.

2 Calculating Forces

2.1 Simple Case

When the vectors in a cross product are in a cartesian coordinate plane (so that the angle ϕ between them is easily computed), the formula F=qvBsinϕ can be used to find the magnitude of F and its direction is given by the right–hand rule.

When the vectors are not in a coordinate plane, finding the angle ϕ between them and the perpendicular direction requires a diagram and several steps of trigonometry. In this case, it is easier to use vector notation to compute the cross product.

2.2 Computing F and F Using Vector Notation

The calculation of the cross product when both vectors in the cross product are along one of the Cartesian coordinate axes is the easiest case (e.g., v=vo​^​ and B=Bo​^). There are two ways of determining the cross-product in this case:

  1. using the right–hand rule with the two unit vectors to determine the direction of F and vB for the magnitude of F or

5 Trajectories

Previously, you computed the force on a moving charged particle in a magnetic field.

As the particle moves, its velocity vector changes. As a result, the direction of force changes. This behavior was addressed when the forces on curved wire segments were computed.

A charged particle moving through a magnetic field will tend to rotate in a circle around the magnetic field lines. There are three general trajectories:

  1. The velocity is parallel to the magnetic field lines. In this case, the particle will continue to move in a straight line because no magnetic forces are acting on it.

  2. The velocity is perpendicular to the magnetic field lines. In this case, the particle motion will be a circle in the same plane as the velocity vector.

  3. The velocity has components that are both parallel and perpendicular to the magnetic field line. In this case, the particle will move at a constant velocity in the direction of the field line and move in a circle around the field line. The path of the particle will be a spiral.

6 Cross product review

Thus far, only the cross product of vectors with one component were considered, e.g.,

v×B=vo​^×Bo​^​

The most general case that you will encounter is

v×B=(vx​^+vy​^​+vz​k^)×(Bx​^+By​^​+Bz​k^)

which has a cross product of

v×B=(vy​Bz​−vz​By​)^+(vz​Bx​−vx​Bz​)^​+(vx​By​−vy​Bx​)k^

This formula is not easy to memorize. There are two ways to derive it

  1. Using the “determinant method”: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2wTUqZa66ng

  2. By splitting up (vx​^+vy​^​+vz​k^)×(Bx​^+By​^​+Bz​k^) into 9 cross products (3 of which will be zero).

When the number of components in v and B is two or less, I recommend using method 2.

6.1 Example

As an example of method 2., consider the case

(vx​^)×(Bx​^+By​^​+Bz​k^)

This can be re-written as (by doing the equivalent of “multiplying through”)

(vx​^×Bx​^)+(vx​^×By​^​)+(vx​^×Bz​k^)

After factoring out the constants, this is

vx​Bx​(^×^)+vx​By​(^×^​)+vx​Bz​(^×k^)

The first term is zero because ^×^=0. The second term is vx​By​k^ because ^×^​=k^. The third term is −vx​Bz​^​ because ^×k^=−^​. Thus,

(vx​^)×(Bx​^+By​^​+Bz​k^)=−vx​By​^​+vx​By​k^

1 LRC Circuits (Ch 31)

Previously, we have examined circuits that are powered by batteries which produce constant voltage (called a direct current, or DC). However, our power distribution system makes use of alternating current (AC) in which the voltages and currents vary sinusoidally with time.

1.1 R Only

The following figure shows a circuit in which an AC power source causes the current to vary sinusoidally in time according to i(t)=Icos(ωt). By Ohm’s law, the equation for the instantaneous voltage across the resistor is vR​(t)=IRcos(ωt).

\include{RLC and AC Circuits/figures/31_07_FigureA.jpg} \include{RLC and AC Circuits/figures/31_07_FigureB.jpg}

We say that the voltage and current are “in phase” because the peaks, valleys, and zero crossings in the plots of i(t) and vR​(t) occur at the same time.

1.2 L Only

As before, the following figure shows a circuit in which an AC power source causes the current to vary sinusoidally in time according to i(t)=Icos(ωt).

In this case, the voltage across the inductor varies according to vL​(t)=IωLcos(ωt+90o).

Because the term ωL occurs frequently, we give it a name of inductive reactance: XL​≡ωL. With this new variable, vL​(t)=IXL​cos(ωt+90∘).

We say the voltage across the inductor “leads” the current by 90∘ (or, equivalently, T/4) because the maxima (or minima) in vR​(t) occur before the maxima (or minima) in i(t). In the following figure, the first minima of the plot of vR​(t) is shown to occur earlier than the first minima in the i(t) plot by T/4 (or equivalently, by 90∘ or π/2).

1.3 C Only

As before, the following figure shows a circuit in which an AC power source causes the current to vary sinusoidally in time according to i(t)=Icos(ωt).

In this case, the voltage across the capacitor varies according to vC​(t)=(I/(ωC))cos(ωt−90o).

Because the term 1/(ωC) occurs frequently, we give it a name of capacitive reactance: XC​≡1/(ωC). With this new variable, vC​(t)=IXC​cos(ωt−90∘).

We say the voltage across the capacitor “lags” the current by 90∘ (or, equivalently, T/4) because the maxima (or minima) in vC​(t) occur after the maxima (or minima) in i(t). 1.4 Series LRC circuit

Suppose that we know the current i(t) in the following series LRC circuit is i(t)=Icos(ωt).

\includegraphics[scale=0.3]{RLC and AC Circuits/figures/31_13_FigureA.jpg}

We want to know the voltage across the AC power supply, vd​−va​, which we call v.

We know the voltage across each of the components from the discussion above.

vR​(t)=IRcos(ωt)

vL​(t)=IωLcos(ωt+90∘)=IXL​cos(ωt+90∘)

vC​(t)=(I/(ωC))cos(ωt−90∘)=IXC​cos(ωt−90∘)

From Ohm’s law, the voltages around a loop must be zero. From this, we have

v(t)=vR​(t)+vL​(t)+vC​(t)

Substitution gives

v(t)=IRcos(ωt)+IXL​cos(ωt+90∘)+IXC​cos(ωt−90∘)

In this activity, you will compute v(t) in two ways. First, you will use the above formula and a trig identity to write v(t) in the form IZcos(ωt+ϕ), where the constants Z and ϕ depend on R, L, and C (or equivalently, R, XL​, and XC​). Next, you will use a general formula to compute Z and ϕ.

1.6 Problem

In the previous problem, computing v(t) required the use of a trig identity to combine vR​ and vL​ and write v(t) in the form v(t)=Zcos(ωt+ϕ), where Z and ϕ are constants that depend on L and R. When vC​ is not zero, additional algebra is needed to compute v(t) (by using the trig identity again). However, there is formula that can be used to find v(t) in general so that trig identities are not needed to compute v(t).

It can be shown that in general, the voltage across the AC power source is

v(t)=IZcos(ωt+ϕ)

Where the series LRC impedance Z is defined as

Z=R2+(XL​−XC​)2​

and the phase angle ϕ of v(t) is defined as

ϕ=tan−1(RXL​−XC​​)=tan−1(RωL−1/(ωC)​)

When ϕ is positive, v(t) leads i(t). When ϕ is negative, v(t) lags i(t). When ϕ=0, v(t) is in phase with i(t).

  1. Using the parameters given in the previous problem, find v(t) using the above formula.

    Answer: In the previous problem, we were given I=1 and ω=2π, IR=1, IXL​=IωL=1, XC​=1/(ωC). From this, we conclude R=1, ωL=1, and 1/(ωC)=0. Thus

    Z=R2+(XL​−XC​)2​=12+(1−0)2​

    and

    ϕ=tan−1(RXL​−XC​​)=tan−1(11−0​)=tan−1(11​)=4π​

    Thus,

    v(t)=IZcos(ωt+ϕ)=2​cos(ωt+π/4)

    which is the same as found in the previous problem.

  2. Does v(t) lead or lag i(t)?

    Answer: Lead. A plot of i(t) and v(t) shows that peaks in v(t) occur before peaks in i(t).

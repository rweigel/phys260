Enclosed Charge

2

1 Overview

5

When using Gauss’s law, one must draw an imaginary volume in space and compute how much charge is inside the volume. Note that “charge enclosed” and “charge inside” are used interchangeably.

7

In general, an imaginary volume is chosen is one for which computation of the electric flux is easy. That is, the imaginary volume will be such that the electric field is either parallel or perpendicular to all parts of the surface. In this activity, you are given the Gaussian volume. However, you should understand the reason for the choice of each Gaussian volume given.

9

2 Densities

11

There are three types of charge densities, linear (λ), surface (σ), and volume (ρ).

13

Given a Q, and assuming Q is uniformly distributed on (or within for a volume) an object, to find

15

  • λ, divide Q by the length over which the charge is spread;

    17

  • σ, divide Q by the area over which the charge is spread; and

    18

  • ρ, divide Q by the volume over which the charge is spread.

    19
17-19

To find Q given a charge density, multiply the charge density by the relevant length, area, or volume.

21

Key formulas:

23

  • 2πR – circumference of a circle

    25

  • 4πR2 – surface area of sphere of radius R

    26

  • 2πRh – surface area of curved part of cylinder of radius R and height h

    27

  • πR2 – surface area of cap of cylinder of radius R

    28

  • πR2h – volume of cylinder cylinder of radius R and height h

    29

  • 4πR3/3 – volume of sphere of radius R

    30
25-30

2.1 Examples

32

  1. A total charge of Q is uniformly distributed on a wire of negligible thickness that forms a circle of radius R. What is the charge density?

    34-35

    Answer The appropriate type of density is a linear charge density, because the charge is effectively distributed along a line. λ=Q/2πR.

    36

  2. A total charge of Q is uniformly distributed on only the curved part of a cylinder of radius R and height h. What is the surface charge density?

    38-39

    Answer: The surface charge density of the curved part is Q divided by the area over which the charge is distributed, which is 2πRh: σ=Q/(2πRh).

    40

2.2 Problems

44

  1. A total charge of Q is uniformly distributed on the two end caps of a closed cylinder of radius R and height h. What is the surface charge density?

    46-47

    Answer: σ=Q/(2πR2) (The factor of 2 because there are two caps, each of area πR2.)

    48-51

  2. A total charge of Q is uniformly distributed on the surface of a sphere of radius R. What is the surface charge density?

    54-55

    Answer: σ=Q/(4πR2)

7 Cylindrical Shell

254

A non–conducting hollow cylinder of radius R and length H has a charge of +3Q uniformly distributed on its curved surface. The Gaussian cylinder shown has a length h, radius r, and the same center line as the charged cylinder.

256
258

  1. Find the linear charge density, λ, of the charged cylinder.

    260-261

    Answer: The linear charge density is the total charge divided by the length over which the charge is distributed: λ=3Q/H.

    Note: The charges are distributed on a surface, so it may seem more natural to use a surface charge density, which is σ=3Q/(2πRH), where the denominator is the surface area of the curved part of the charged cylinder. In this case, the answer to 2. does not change and the answer to 3. is Qencl​=σ2πRh instead of Qencl​=λh. We use charge per length in this problem because the formula for Qencl​ is simpler.

    262-266
    268-269

  2. Find an equation for Qencl​ for r<R.

    272-273

    Answer: If the Gaussian cylinder is fully inside the hollow cylinder, there would be no charge inside of it. As a result, the charge enclosed for r<R is zero: Qencl​=0.

    274-276
    278-279

  3. Find an equation for Qencl​ for r>R. Your equation should involve λ and one or more of h, r, and R.

    282-283

    Answer: The amount of charge enclosed is the charge per unit length ×h=λh, so Qencl​=λh. (The amount of charge enclosed does not depend on r.)

    284-286
    288-289

  4. Find the amount of charge enclosed by the Gaussian cylinder when it has radii of r=0, r=R/2, r=2R, and r=3R.

    292-293

    Answer: 0,0,λh,λh

    316-317

    318-320
    322-323

    326-327

    Answer: When r<R, the Gaussian cylinder is entirely inside the charged cylinder. The charge in the Gaussian cylinder is the charge density of the charged cylinder times the volume of the Gaussian cylinder: Qencl​(r)=ρπr2h.

    342-343
